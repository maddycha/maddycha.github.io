Amazon Homepage Redesign

Work & Co Design Challenge

Skills:

ui/ux design

user research

prototyping

Overview:
Despite being the world's largest online retailer, Amazon struggles with information hierarchy and visual identity. This is incredibly apparent in the design of its home page.

For this project, I focused on improving the experience for frequent users of Amazon Prime with the goal of creating larger basket sizes per prime customer and increasing product subscriptions for Amazon. I had seven days to complete this project.

Problem

The amount of information on the Amazon home page is incredibly overwhelming, and deters users from browsing.

User Research

For this project, I conducted five semi-structured interviews with people that fit the intended user group—frequent users of Amazon Prime. At this stage, I was mostly interested in understanding their motivations for using Amazon Prime, their shopping habits within the website, and how they come across new products. I created an affinity diagram to better analyze the data I collected.




Customer Journey Map

I also worked on a very rudimentary customer journey map to better illustrate the way that users shop through Amazon. This helped me better understand the problem space and develop goals for myself for the project. I wanted to make sure to help alleviate some of the pain points within the home page to motivate people to spend time on it, rather than only relying on the search functionality. This would ideally help to increase basket size, as users would be browsing (and ideally purchasing) more items than intended.

Customer Types

Through my interviews, I identified two main customer types: people who are on Amazon for very specific products and people shopping without a specific product/goal in mind.

Customer Needs

Out of the five people I interviewed, none of them relied on the homepage for their shopping needs. Despite not always knowing what to purchase, customers found the homepage to be incredibly overwhelming and hard to navigate. To remedy this, I created a list of user needs that the homepage would need to fulfill:

  • more context on the homepage (in terms of item listing and why it's being recommended)
  • less "random clutter" in between recommendation
  • more straightforward navigation through pages

Competitive Analysis

Before iterating, I decided to look at how different retailers structure their homepages and what content they included. I looked into various websites, with a focus on retailers that sold a variety of items. A big similarity that these websites had (with Amazon as well) was the amount of cognitive load throughout the page. I tried to take note of how different pages balanced content and empty space (or lack of).

Components

Based on my insights, I decided that I wanted my redesign to include components that would be personalized based on user's shopping and browsing history.

Through components, I would be able to target the different needs of the two customer types I identified, while not overloading users with extraneous information.

  • suggested categores
  • subscribe and save promotion (if user has re-purchasing shopping habits)
  • recommendations based on purchase/browsing history
  • specific component for advertisements
  • deal of the day items

Low-fidelity Screens

I began brainstorming through creating low-fidelity screens. At this point, I had a general idea of the content I wanted to include, but I still needed to figure out how I would structure everything while not overwhelming the user.

Mid-fidelity Screens

To better illustrate my concept, I worked on mid-fidelity screens that could be tested with the users I had interviewed in the beginning of the process. I ended up with two variants of the same idea, with differences in layout and planned interaction.

User Feedback

I asked the users I interviewed with for some feedback on my designs, with an emphasis on the content o the homepage as well as the planned interactivity. Additionally, I asked users about what sort of sections they would be interested in seeing. These are the notes from my testing sessions:

  • preferred having a simple vertical scroll w/ no horizontal scrolling
  • really appreciated being able to filter recommended items by categories
  • wanted to know how hamburger menu would work/how navigation would be redesigned
  • liked being able to see how different items/purchasing plans would save them money
  • appreciated copy giving more context for different listings appearing on homepage

Design Guidelines

I created a very simple design guideline for myself as I went through the redesign. As one of the issues I found through my research was that the current design was incredibly overwhelming, I stuck to very simple typography. I also tried to stick very closely to the current visual style of Amazon (aside from their commissioned font, which I substituted with Neue Haas Unica for the purpose of this case study).

High-fidelity Screens

With the feedback I received, I began to design out high-fidelity screens to better visualize my concept. I decided to design the screens to be easy to interact with, and ordered components in a way that would increase visual variability. I made sure to include real content to make the whole experience seem more concrete.

Prototyping

Once I was satisfied with my high-fidelity designs, I imported them into Origami to highlight some of the interactions I had in mind. From here, I refined the interactions I had in mind and made suree that each interaction seemed intuitive enough. I tried to keep the motions consistent within different interactions.

Final Design

I prototyped out interactions that I thought would be essential to the designs, such as opening the hamburger menu, checking tool tips, and navigating through different sections within the recommended items.

Reflection

All in all, I learned a lot about rapidly prototyping and balancing research and design through this project. If I had more time, I would have loved to conduct more user research, and possibly prototype out pages that would compliment the home page. Additionally, I would love to design out each of the potential components so that it would be easier to hypothetically implement the system.
